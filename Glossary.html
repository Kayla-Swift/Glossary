Assess patient, wellbeing and wound

Establish diagnosis and baseline characteristics for appropriate support and comorbidities that may impact healing. Record wound type, location, size, wound bed condition, signs of infection / inflammation, pain location and intensity, comorbidities, adherence / concordance to treatment.

Bring in multi-disciplinary team and informal carers to promote holistic patient care.

Record referral to others such as surgical team, wound specialist nurse, dietician, pain team, vascular and diabetes team, podiatrist, physiotherapist, family carers and trained counsellor.

Control or treat underlying causes and barriers to wound healing

Record management plan for: systemic infection, diabetes, nutritional problems, oedema, continence, mobility, vascular issues, pain, stress, anxiety, non-adherence / concordance with offloading and compression, lifestyle choices

Decide appropriate treatment

Pressure Injury

A pressure injury due to intense/unrelieved pressure and shear forces at the bone-tendon-muscle interface. DTPIs present as persistent and non-blanching localized areas with dark red, maroon, or purple discoloration, the overlying skin may or may not be intact. There may also be epidermal separation present revealing a dark-colored wound bed or a blood blister.

McNichol, L. L., Ratliff, C. R., and Yates, S. S. (2022). Wound, ostomy, and Continence Nurses Society Core Curriculum. Wolters Kluwer.

Content Source Image Credit

A stage 1 pressure injury is non-blanchable erythema of intact skin. It is a localized area of skin discoloration of intact skin over a bony prominence with non-blanchable erythema (does not include purple or maroon discoloration as these indicate deep tissue pressure injury).

McNichol, L. L., Ratliff, C. R., and Yates, S. S. (2022). Wound, ostomy, and Continence Nurses Society Core Curriculum. Wolters Kluwer.

Content Source Image Credit

A stage 2 pressure injury is partial-thickness skin loss with exposed dermis. It is a partial-thickness ulcer with damage to the epidermis and part of the dermis, the dermis is exposed and the wound bed is viable, pink/red, moist, and may present as a blister. Adipost tissue and deeper tissues are not visible and granulation tissue, slough, and eschar are not present.

McNichol, L. L., Ratliff, C. R., and Yates, S. S. (2022). Wound, ostomy, and Continence Nurses Society Core Curriculum. Wolters Kluwer.

Content Source Image Credit

A stage 3 pressure injury is a full-thickness skin loss. It is an ulcer extending into the underlying subcutaneous tissue where adipose tissue is visible. Granulation tissue, rolled wound edges (epibole), slough, eschar, tunneling, and undermining may also be present. Underlying fascia, muscle, tendon, logament, cartilage, or bone are not exposed.

McNichol, L. L., Ratliff, C. R., and Yates, S. S. (2022). Wound, ostomy, and Continence Nurses Society Core Curriculum. Wolters Kluwer.

Content Source Image Credit

A stage 4 pressure injury is a full-thickness skin and tissue loss. It is an ulcer extending into the underlying fascia, muscle, tendon, ligament, cartilage, and/or bone - where these structures are exposed or directly palpable.

McNichol, L. L., Ratliff, C. R., and Yates, S. S. (2022). Wound, ostomy, and Continence Nurses Society Core Curriculum. Wolters Kluwer.
